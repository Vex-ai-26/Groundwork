## **DAY 5 - Reddit**
**Platform:** r/contractors  
**Action:** Follow-up educational post  
**Title:** "How I teach high schoolers to estimate construction jobs (system you can steal)"

**Copy:**
I taught construction for years after running crews, and the hardest thing to teach isn't tool skills—it's estimating. These kids need to understand real-world costs before they graduate.

Here's the dead-simple system I use in class (works for actual jobs too):

**Step 1: Material Takeoff**
Go room by room or system by system. Write down EVERYTHING. Studs, drywall, screws, mud, tape, corner bead. If you think "I'll remember that," you won't.

**Step 2: Separate Labor from Materials**
Don't lump them together. You need to see both margins. Materials might be 40% of the job, but labor is where you actually make money—or lose it.

**Step 3: Add the Stuff Everyone Forgets**
- Waste factor (I use 10% on materials)
- Dump fees
- Permits
- Your drive time if it's far

**Step 4: Build Your Proposal Around It**
Client doesn't need to see your takeoff sheet. They need a clean proposal with clear line items and a total.

I made templates for all this because I got sick of reinventing the wheel every semester. Same ones I used in the field for years. Just put them on Gumroad for anyone who wants a plug-and-play system.

But whether you use mine or build your own, the key is: use the SAME system every time. That's how you stop under-bidding.
