**Platform:** r/personalfinance

**Title:** The "10% visibility rule" changed how I actually hit my savings goals

**Copy:**
Used to set savings goals every January. Failed by March. Every time.

The problem wasn't discipline—it was visibility. I'd check my savings account once a month, see a number that meant nothing without context, and lose motivation.

Started using what I call the "10% visibility rule": break every goal into 10 milestones, track each one visually.

**Example:** $5,000 emergency fund becomes ten $500 chunks. Hit $500? That's 10% done. Visual progress bar moves. You SEE the win.

**Why 10% specifically?** 
- Small enough to hit quickly (dopamine boost)
- Large enough to feel meaningful
- Easy math for any goal amount

**What I track for each goal:**
- Target amount and deadline
- Current progress (as a percentage)
- Required monthly contribution to hit deadline
- Last deposit date (keeps me honest)

Been doing this 18 months. Fully funded my emergency fund, saved for a used truck, and halfway to a house down payment. First time I've ever stuck with savings goals past 6 months.

The simple shift: I stopped tracking money and started tracking progress.

Anyone else find that seeing progress matters more than seeing dollar amounts?

## **DAY 5 - REDDIT (Follow-up Value Post)**

**Platform:** r/povertyfinance

**Action:** Create text post with actionable savings framework

**Title:** Saved my first $1,000 using the "milestone method" – sharing the exact system

**Copy:**
Never had $1,000 saved before last year. I'm 34.

Every time I tried, something would come up, or I'd lose motivation around $300-$400 and raid the account for something stupid.

**What changed:** I stopped tracking the dollar amount and started tracking milestones.

**The system:**
- Broke $1,000 into 10 milestones ($100 each)
- Every time I hit a milestone, I marked it complete
- Tracked the percentage (30% feels way better than "$300")

**Why this worked for me:**
Hitting $100 felt like a WIN. Hitting $200 felt like momentum. By $500, I was obsessed with finishing.

Before, $300 felt like "not enough to matter anyway" so I'd spend it. With milestones, $300 meant "I'm 30% done" and I protected it differently.

**I also tracked:**
- Date I hit each milestone (kept me accountable)
- How much I needed to save per paycheck to hit $1,000 in 6 months ($42/paycheck for me)
- Next milestone target date

Now I'm at $1,850 in emergency fund and saving for a car. Same method, different goals.

If you're stuck around a few hundred bucks and keep dipping into savings, try milestones instead of dollar amounts. Changed everything for me.
