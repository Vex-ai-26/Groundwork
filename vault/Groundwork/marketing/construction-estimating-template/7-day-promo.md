## **DAY 2 – REDDIT (Teaching Post)**

**Platform:** r/SweatyStartup  
**Action:** Create text post teaching the "10-10 rule" with practical example

**Post Title:**  
"If you're not using the 10-10 rule on estimates, you're probably working for free"

**Post Copy:**

Took me way too long to figure this out.

The **10-10 rule** = 10% overhead + 10% profit, calculated the right way.

Here's what most new contractors do wrong:

Let's say your job costs are $5,000 (labor + materials).

❌ **WRONG WAY:**  
Add 10% overhead: $5,000 + $500 = $5,500  
Add 10% profit: $5,500 + $550 = $6,050

✅ **RIGHT WAY:**  
Calculate overhead on cost: $5,000 × 1.10 = $5,500  
Calculate profit on new subtotal: $5,500 × 1.10 = $6,050  
**Wait, same number?**

Actually, no. Here's why it matters on real numbers:

If your cost is $8,240:
- Wrong way total: $9,910  
- Right way total: $9,970  

You just left $60 on the table. Do that on 50 jobs a year, that's $3,000 in profit you didn't capture.

The real issue: most guys don't calculate it at all. They just "add 20% to feel safe." Then wonder why they're busy but broke.

**What actually needs to go in overhead?**  
- Insurance (liability, workers comp)  
- Truck payment, fuel, maintenance  
- Tools and equipment  
- Phone, software, advertising  
- Taxes and accounting  

Add yours up. Divide by annual revenue. That's your real overhead percentage. For most small contractors, it's 12-18%, not 10%.

Profit is separate. That's the money you get paid for the risk of running the business.

If you're not breaking these out on every single estimate, you're guessing. And guessing doesn't pay the mortgage.

What's your actual overhead percentage? Most guys have no idea.
