Starter Tools Guide

8 min read
beginner
by Sam · Groundwork

The biggest mistake new builders make is going to Home Depot and buying everything that looks useful. You end up with a pile of tools you don't need and a lighter wallet. Here's the real starter list — what you actually need, what to skip, and how to spend smart.

the rule

Buy a tool when a project requires it. Not before. This keeps your spending tied to actual work and prevents you from accumulating stuff that sits in a drawer.

Must-have tools — buy these first

📏
Tape Measure must have
The most used tool on any job site. Get a 25-foot tape — long enough for most jobs, short enough to carry comfortably. Look for a wide blade that stands out on its own (blade standout matters more than people think).
budget: $15–25 brand: Stanley or Milwaukee
🔨
Hammer must have
A 16 oz framing hammer is the standard. Heavy enough to drive nails, light enough not to wear you out. Don't buy a cheap plastic-handled one — the handle absorbs shock and cheap ones crack.
budget: $20–35 brand: Estwing
🪛
Drill / Driver must have
A cordless drill is the most versatile power tool you'll own. Get a combo kit — drill and impact driver together. 18V or 20V lithium ion. You'll use this on almost every single project.
budget: $80–150 combo brand: DeWalt, Milwaukee, or Ryobi
📐
Speed Square must have
A small triangular square for marking 90 and 45 degree angles. Fits in your pocket, costs almost nothing, and you'll reach for it constantly. Also works as a fence for circular saw cuts.
budget: $8–15 brand: Swanson
🫧
Level must have
A 24-inch level handles most jobs. Use it for hanging anything on a wall, checking if a surface is flat, and plumbing vertical work. Cheap levels are inaccurate — spend a little more here.
budget: $25–40 brand: Empire or Stabila
✏️
Pencil + Chalk Line must have
Always mark before you cut. A carpenter's pencil for close work, a chalk line for snapping long straight lines across lumber or subflooring. Both are cheap and essential.
budget: $5–12 total

Good to have — buy when needed

🪚
Circular Saw buy when needed
Your first real cutting tool. A 7-1/4 inch circular saw cuts lumber, plywood, and decking. Buy it when your first project requires more than straight hand cuts. Corded is fine to start.
budget: $60–100 brand: DeWalt or Skilsaw
🗜️
Clamps buy when needed
Clamps are your extra hands. You'll need them for woodworking and assembly. Start with a set of 4 quick-release clamps in various sizes. You'll slowly accumulate more — every woodworker does.
budget: $20–40 for a starter set
🔧
Utility Knife buy when needed
Scoring drywall, trimming material, opening boxes. A retractable blade utility knife is a job site staple. Get one with a quick blade change — you'll use new blades more than you think.
budget: $10–18

Skip these for now

✕
Combo tool kits from discount stores
20-piece tool sets for $49 look like a deal. They're not. The tools are inaccurate, break fast, and teach you bad habits. Buy one good tool instead.
✕
Miter saw (to start)
Great tool, but expensive and not necessary until you're doing trim work or repeat angle cuts. A circular saw handles most beginner cuts just fine.
✕
Air compressor and nail gun
You don't need this yet. Hand nailing teaches you fundamentals and a hammer works perfectly for most beginner projects. Add this when you're framing walls regularly.
✕
Laser level
Cool but unnecessary. A standard bubble level does everything you need starting out. Laser levels become useful when you're doing large-scale layout work.
🛒
buying tip

Facebook Marketplace and Craigslist are gold for tools. Contractors sell off old equipment constantly and you can get professional-grade tools for beginner prices. Look for name brands only — no off-brand deals.
